Chapter 6: Deviance & Social Control

What Is Social Deviance?

Social deviance: any transgression of socially established norms. It is normative (based on norms) and relativist (differs across place, time, and within societies).

Includes (all?) crime. What counts as deviant changes across cultures and eras.

Functionalist Perspectives

Durkheim

While deviance is abnormal, a certain amount is functional — it contributes to social cohesion by reinforcing the sense of what is right and wrong.

Anomie: a state in which society's norms fail to regulate behavior.

Social Bond Theory (Hirschi)

Asks: "Why don't people commit crime?" Four bonds hold people to conventional society:

  • Attachment (to others)
  • Commitment (to conventional activities)
  • Involvement (in conventional activities)
  • Belief (in society's values and rules)

Conflict Perspective

Class-dominant theory: the dominant class's interests determine what is labeled deviant or criminal.

Feminist Perspective

  • Most research done by and about males
  • Differences in socialization shape deviance patterns
  • Stigmatization differs by gender
  • Women victimized by virtue of gender

Symbolic Interactionism

*Primary vs. Secondary Deviance (Short Answer): Primary = initial rule-breaking. Secondary = person internalizes the "deviant" label and it becomes part of their identity, producing further deviance.

Differential Association Theory

Deviant behavior results from associating with people who have attitudes favorable to deviance. Depends on:

  • Frequency
  • Length
  • Intensity
  • Chronology of relationships

Incarceration & Criminal Justice

*3 Explanations for Rising Incarceration (Short Answer):
1. Mandatory minimum sentencing
2. "Three strikes" laws
3. War on Drugs
These also drive racial discrimination in policing and the criminal justice system.
Key trend: Incarceration rates rose dramatically even as crime rates declined. The prison population surged due to policy changes (mandatory minimums, three strikes, War on Drugs), not increases in crime.

Racial disparities: Higher arrest rates even when offending is comparable (e.g., drug possession, drug dealing); over-policing of impoverished urban neighborhoods; whites less likely to be arrested, charged, found guilty.

Reading: Situational Ethics & Cheating

Helps understand techniques of neutralization. Not independently testable.

Chapter 7: Class & Inequality

Principles of Stratification

*4 Principles (Short Answer):
1. Trait of society, not individual differences
2. Carries over from generation to generation
3. Universal but variable
4. Involves not just inequality but beliefs as well

Caste vs. Class

Caste SocietyClass Society
Closed system — remain at birth level for lifeOpen system — upward/downward mobility possible
Tight rules on relationships (e.g., India's Varnas, U.S. slavery)Based on income, occupation, education

General teardrop pattern: small elite at top, bulging lower-middle/working class, tapering at very bottom.

Functionalist: Davis & Moore

Most important positions require more skill and suffer scarcity of personnel. Society rewards to ensure best fill important jobs. → Meritocracy

Critique: May limit discovery of talent — those born to privilege get fuller opportunities; others limited by poor schooling, little money, lack of networks.

Conflict: Marx

Conflict between workers and capitalists. Those with power use stratification to favor themselves and their children, even if detrimental to lower classes.

Income & Wealth

Income: money received for services/investments. Wealth (net worth): assets minus debts. Wealth inequality is far greater than income inequality.

Neighborhood Poverty & Food Deserts

Poor neighborhoods: more negative social, economic, educational effects (rural, suburban, AND urban). Higher risk of heart disease, diabetes, cancer, obesity. Food deserts: areas lacking access to affordable, healthy food.

Chapter 8: Gender & Society

Sex vs. Gender

Sex: anatomical/biological differences from genes. Gender: social concept linked to norms, roles, behaviors — a social construct. Both are increasingly seen as fluid.

Functionalist: Parsons' Sex Role Theory

Complementary roles: women as mothers/caregivers, men as breadwinners. Worried about negative consequences of social change.

Critique: Neglects power differentials from economic dependence. Not commonly argued in contemporary sociology.

Conflict & Feminist Views

Conflict: Patriarchal capitalists benefit through subordination of women.

Feminist: "Men produce and women reproduce." Private vs. public spheres give males privileged access to money, status, independence, opportunity.

Agents of Socialization

Family: Roots vs. Wings

Daughters given "roots" (protection, closeness) and sons given "wings" (independence, exploration).

Peers: Social Dosage Effect

Encouragement/support vs. aggressive/competitive play. Gender policing stigmatizes those who perform gender "incorrectly."

Doing Gender (West & Zimmerman, 1987)

Gender is not a static identity but an activity. Creates differences that, while not biological, appear natural because consistently enacted.

Gender Inequality

Second Shift (Hochschild): unpaid housework after paid employment. Marriage has "traditionalizing" effect.

Gender Wage Gap: women earn ~$0.85 per male dollar; disparity increases for women of color.

*Glass Ceiling: unofficial barrier women face advancing to upper levels of organizations.
*Glass Escalator: promotional boost men gain in predominantly female occupations (specific to men in female-dominated careers).

Feminism

Black feminist critique & double jeopardy: early liberal feminism was about white middle-class women. Double jeopardy = discrimination on both race AND gender.

Hegemonic masculinity: culturally normative idea emphasizing strength, domination, aggression. Harmful for both men and women.

Marriage Trends

  • Rates declined; fewer see marriage as necessary
  • Divorce peaked in 70s-80s
  • Obergefell v. Hodges (2015): same-sex marriage
  • Coontz: "traditional" nuclear family was specific to a time/population, not historically dominant

Readings

Marked (Tannen): Women in the workplace are always "marked" — their choices carry extra meaning men's don't.
The Way We Weren't (Coontz): Be familiar with class discussion.

Chapter 9: Race & Ethnicity

Race as Social Construct

Race: a group sharing apparent physical traits deemed socially significant. A social concept, NOT a scientific one.
Thomas Theorem: "If people define situations as real, they are real in their consequences."

Race vs. Ethnicity

RaceEthnicity
Based on perceived physical traitsShared cultural heritage (language, origin, religion)

Minority: less powerful group dominated politically and economically by the dominant group.

Discrimination

Individual discrimination: overt, intentional unequal treatment based on prejudice.
Institutional discrimination: discrimination enshrined in law, policy, or common practice (redlining, War on Drugs, segregation laws).

Housing & Wealth

Redlining: refusing loans/insurance in minority areas → prevented homeownership/wealth building.

Blockbusting: exploiting racial fears to buy cheap, resell at inflated prices to minorities.

Post-WWII policies promoted homeownership limited to whites. Discrimination continues via subprime lending.

Race: Power of an Illusion

Thind & Ozawa cases: Ozawa (1922) argued whiteness by skin color — denied (must be "Caucasian"). Thind (1923) argued he was Caucasian — denied ("common understanding"). Shows arbitrary, constructed nature of racial categories.

Different states' policies: States used varying and inconsistent rules to define race (e.g., "one-drop" rules, different blood quantum thresholds). This further demonstrates that race is a social/legal construct, not a biological one.

Color-blind Ideology

Ignoring race allows existing systemic inequalities to persist unchallenged. MLK's dream was about not being judged by color, not ignoring it. "To ignore color is to ignore reality."

Readings

Optional Ethnicities (Waters): For white Americans, ethnicity is often optional and symbolic (consequence-free). For minorities, ethnicity shapes daily life.
Racial Formation & Colorblind readings: Not independently testable.

Chapter 10: The State, Power & Economy

Citizenship Rights

*Civil vs. Social Rights (Short Answer):
Civil rights: guarantee personal freedom WITHOUT state interference (speech, religion, assembly).
Social rights: guarantee protection BY the state (education, healthcare, welfare).
Political rights: ensure citizens can participate in governance (voting).

Functionalist View

Modern societies may lack consensus. Government as neutral body mediating competing interests.

Conflict: Marx

Government represents and serves capitalist class interests. Power concentrated in hands of few.

Class Dominance Theory & Power Elite

Power elite (Mills): revolving door between government, business, and military. Domhoff: corporate/business elite holds the most influence. Power indicators: Who governs? Who wins?
